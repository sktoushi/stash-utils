Cost of Reducing Inflation

Economists often disagree on the cost of reducing inflation. The standard view, associated with the short-run Phillips curve, suggests that reducing inflation requires a period of low output and high unemployment.

The cost is measured by the sacrifice ratio, which is the number of percentage points of GDP that must be forgone to reduce inflation by one percentage point.

Alternative Views

  • Some economists believe the cost can be smaller than what the sacrifice ratio indicates, particularly if the disinflationary policy is announced in advance and is credible.
  • Others think the cost is much higher, arguing that a recession caused by disinflation might raise the natural rate of unemployment, leading to a more persistent problem.

Because of these differing views, economists provide conflicting advice to policymakers on how to manage inflation effectively.

Government Budget Deficits

In the U.S., large budget deficits between 1980 and 1995 caused the government debt to GDP ratio to double. Although the budget came under control by the late 1990s, deficits reemerged in the 2000s due to war, recession, and policy changes.

Long-term Fiscal Concerns

Many economists worry that deficits will grow larger as the baby-boom generation retires, leading to increased strain on Social Security and Medicare.

Traditional View

Most economists believe that running a budget deficit reduces national savings, which leads to lower investment, a trade deficit, smaller capital stock, and larger foreign debt. This view holds that government debt burdens future generations.
