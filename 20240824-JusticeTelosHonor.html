Justice, Telos, and Honor

The text discusses Aristotle's theory of justice, focusing on two key ideas:

  1. Justice is teleological: This means that determining rights requires understanding the telos (purpose, end, or essential nature) of a social practice. In other words, we need to figure out what the purpose of the practice is in order to determine what is just.
  2. Justice is honorific: Reasoning about the telos of a practice also involves reasoning about what virtues that practice should honor and reward. Justice, in this sense, is connected to what is valued and what is honored in a given society.

The text further explains how modern theories of justice often attempt to separate questions of fairness and rights from arguments about honor, virtue, and moral desert (what people deserve). However, Aristotle's view is that debates about justice are, by their nature, debates about honor, virtue, and the nature of the good life.

Equity and Compensation

Aristotle's approach to justice involves giving people what they deserve, which is tied to their relevant virtues and contributions. This perspective on justice is contrasted with more modern, utilitarian views, where the focus might be on producing the greatest happiness for the greatest number rather than on honoring individual merit and virtue.
